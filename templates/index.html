ℹ️ About this data & caveats

TRI data is self-reported annually by facilities above certain size and chemical-use thresholds. It undercounts — small emitters and non-covered industries aren't included. Releases being reported doesn't necessarily mean they're illegal or unsafe; it means the facility disclosed them as required by law. Quantities are pounds released per year. Source: EPA Toxics Release Inventory via Envirofacts.

      Each dot is one Michigan county.

      Rural county Urban county (large population) ? why this matters
      Association is not causation. A trend here does not prove one thing causes the other. Counties differ in many ways — population, age, industry, smoking, income — that also affect health. Use this as a starting point for questions, not an answer.

      ℹ️ Important context — click to expand
      • Urban counties (Wayne / Oakland / Macomb / Genesee / Saginaw) have high asthma rates driven by air pollution, poverty, housing conditions, and industrial emissions — not agricultural pesticides.
      • Asthma and COPD have many causes: smoking, indoor allergens, air quality, genetics, occupational exposures, socioeconomic factors.
      • Pesticide applicators have direct occupational exposure risk; that differs from ambient community exposure.
      • Pesticide and respiratory data are annual and county-level. Monthly seasonal correlation isn't possible at the county grain.
      • Broader ICD-10 categories (J00-J06, J20-J22, J60-J70) use Michigan statewide baselines because no county-level breakdowns are published.
      • This tool is for exploratory visualization only — it does not constitute epidemiological evidence.

      ℹ️ Important context — click to expand
      • Cancer has long latency (10–30 years). Current rates may reflect exposures from decades ago, not today's application levels.
      • The pesticide data (through ~2019) and NCI cancer data (2018–2022) cover overlapping but not identical periods.
      • This is an ecological comparison — it compares county populations, not individuals. Individual exposure data would be needed for causal links (the ecological fallacy).
      • Major confounders: smoking, alcohol, obesity, occupational/industrial exposure, socioeconomic status, healthcare/screening access, age structure, and other contaminants (PFAS, industrial pollution).
      • Urban counties have different cancer profiles for reasons unrelated to agriculture — urban/rural stratification is essential.
      • Some agricultural counties may have lower overall cancer rates (younger, healthier, lower-smoking populations) — this does not mean pesticides are protective.
      • Data suppression in low-population counties (<16 cases) creates gaps that can bias the analysis.
      • This tool is for exploratory visualization only. It does not constitute epidemiological evidence of causation.

      Pesticide–cancer evidence

      Literature-based associations (IARC monographs + Agricultural Health Study). This is the scientific basis behind the compounds shown in the matrix — separate from the ecological correlations computed from Michigan county data.

      IARC groups: 1 = carcinogenic; 2A = probably; 2B = possibly; 3 = not classifiable. Evidence strength reflects epidemiological weight, not regulatory status.

      Welcome — start here

      This is a free tool for exploring Michigan's environmental and health data on one map. It brings together so you can look for patterns and ask better questions.

      🗺️

      Explore the map

      Use Map layers (left) to color counties by pesticide use, cancer, respiratory health, contamination, or industrial toxic releases (TRI), and to stack overlays like water sites, factory markers, and wind. Hover anything with a ? for an explanation.

      📊

      Compare two things

      The Explore correlations tab lets you pick any two measures — say, herbicide use vs. a cancer rate — and see whether they rise and fall together across counties, explained in plain English.

      🔎

      What the data is

      Everything comes from public sources (USGS, EPA, CDC, NCI, Michigan DNR/EGLE). Open Data sources any time to see each dataset, how current it is, and its limitations.

      About this project. This is an independent, non-commercial educational project that compiles publicly available data from USGS, EPA, CDC, NCI, USDA, and Michigan state agencies onto one map. It is not affiliated with or endorsed by any of them. It exists to make public environmental and health data easier to explore and to help people ask better-informed questions.

      Please read: This tool is for exploration and education only. Any patterns it shows are associations, not proof of cause — counties differ in age, income, industry, smoking, and many other ways that affect health. Nothing here is medical, legal, regulatory, or scientific advice. Do not use it to draw conclusions about any individual person, place, or product.

      Data sources

      All data is fetched live from the sources listed below. Where a source is unavailable or requires registration, it is marked accordingly and excluded from the map until configured. The Coverage column shows the date range each layer's data spans; Last updated shows when it was last refreshed.

      Data limitations: USGS EPest values are estimates derived from proprietary sale data and crop-extent models. Low/high bracket the uncertainty — see USGS Data Series 907 for methodology. County totals include only crop-protection use; lawn, golf, and aquatic non-agricultural use are not included.

      Pesticide use: USGS NAWQA EPest · Boundaries: US Census TIGER · Crops: USDA NASS · Regulator: MDARD
      For research and educational use. Pesticide-use figures are county-level estimates with documented uncertainty. USGS publishes values in kilograms; this app converts to pounds (1 kg = 2.20462 lbs) for display.
